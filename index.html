Abbe Refractometer

Principle and Application of the Pulfrich-Refractometer

Principles of Refraction

Refraction is the bending of light during transmission from one transparent substance into another, caused by different speeds of light propagation. The incident ray is refracted at a certain angle. When the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, the ray is reflected back into the medium; this is known as Total Internal Reflection.

Abbe vs. Pulfrich

The Abbe Refractometer uses the principle of the critical angle and total reflection to determine the refractive index. A shadow boundary is created between a measurement and illumination prism. The Pulfrich Refractometer uses a V-prism where light enters horizontally at a grazing angle, and the deflection angle is measured rather than the critical angle.

Surveying Instruments

Principle and Application of Surveying Instruments

Theodolite and Tachymeter

A Theodolite is an instrument used to measure horizontal and vertical angles using graduated circles and a telescope. A Tachymeter is essentially a theodolite with an integrated distance measurement system, allowing for the determination of direction, distance, and height in one step.

Floor Area Calculation

To calculate the area of a room, the space was partitioned into 5 sections. Using a Total Station, distances and angles were measured from specific set points. The area was calculated by summing these partitioned sections, resulting in a total floor area of 79.7135 m².

Matlab + Simulink Model of an Industrial Cooling Loop

Dynamic simulation of a water–glycol cooling circuit for an industrial drivetrain test facility using Simscape Fluids and Simulink.

Project Context and Objectives

The goal of this model is to size and tune a closed-loop cooling system that removes heat from test-bench components while keeping supply temperatures and pressures inside defined limits.

The plant is represented with Simscape Fluids blocks for pumps, valves, pipework, and a plate heat exchanger, while supervisory logic and PID controllers are implemented in Simulink.

System Topology and Main Flow Path

The circuit consists of a main circulation pump, distribution manifold, consumer branches, a plate heat exchanger connected to a secondary chiller loop, and an expansion vessel. The working fluid is a water–glycol mixture and all components are configured for the expected flow of the test bench.

Detailed Plant Modelling

The cooling plant is decomposed into reusable subsystems for the pump group, mixing header, heat exchanger, and consumer branches. Each subsystem exposes key parameters such as nominal flow rate, pressure rise, heat-transfer area, and valve authority.

Control Architecture

The loop uses a cascaded control structure: pump speed is commanded from a header-pressure PID, while individual consumer-valve controllers regulate outlet temperature or flow.

The supervisory layer provides different operating modes for start-up, steady-state operation, and ramped heat-load tests used during the workshop.

Workshop Scenarios and Results

Several standard scenarios are defined: step changes in heat load, gradual flow ramps, and disturbance tests on the secondary-side cooling capacity. For each case the model records inlet and outlet temperatures, pump speed, valve positions, and pressure drops.

Takeaways

The workshop model demonstrates how Simscape Fluids and Simulink can be used to reason about pressure losses, controller tuning, and thermal transients before hardware is available. The same structure can be adapted to future drivetrain test facilities with different pumps, hose diameters, and cooling capacities.

Thermal Hydraulic Modeling and Control of a Wind-Turbine Cooling Circuit

Detailed Simulink/Simscape implementation of a water–glycol cooling loop for turbine converters and transformer, with wind and power functions.

Project Context and Objectives

Utility-scale wind turbines use liquid-cooled converters and transformers in the nacelle or tower. The cooling circuit must keep component temperatures within limits under changing wind speed, ambient conditions and electrical loading, while respecting pump-head and pressure-drop constraints.

This model represents a complete water–glycol loop, coupled to MATLAB functions for wind-speed scheduling and turbine power. Objectives:

• size plate coolers, pump and valves
• predict converter and transformer inlet temperatures
• evaluate pressure drops and flow balancing
• validate governing equations against Simscape results.

System Architecture

The top-level model contains subsystems for the pumping unit, converters, transformer, plate coolers and an air/utility block for power and air properties. Liquid-side pressure losses and cooler bypass paths are explicit.

2 main heat sources (converters + transformer)
2 plate coolers in parallel
18 operating scenarios swept

Cooler Subsystems

The cooler group contains two plate coolers in parallel with separate pressure-loss elements. Sensors provide inlet and outlet temperatures and pressures for both branches.

Transformer and Converter Branches

Heat sources—two power converters and a transformer—are modelled as distributed loss blocks with branch-specific pressure losses. Loss inputs are computed from turbine electrical power and efficiency and injected into the coolant via thermal ports.

Pumping Unit and Pressure Relief

The pumping unit is a variable-speed centrifugal pump controlled on discharge pressure. A pressure-relief valve returns liquid to the suction side when pressure exceeds a safe limit, protecting the circuit during start-up or valve misconfiguration.

Wind Environment and Power Model

Cooling demand and available convective capacity depend on wind conditions. Two MATLAB functions drive the simulation:

• wind_steps(t,...) – a time-dependent wind-speed schedule.
• wind_turbine_power(V) – a piecewise power curve mapping wind speed to electrical power.

The report documents governing equations for mass and momentum conservation, energy balance, heat-exchanger NTU relations and valve characteristics, then shows how they are implemented in MATLAB and Simscape.

Simulation Campaign and Results

The model is run for 18 operating scenarios combining wind-speed profiles, ambient temperatures and valve settings. For each scenario the script logs converter inlet temperatures, cooler heat duty and pump operating points.

> 5 °C margin to converter temperature limit
~220 kW peak cooler duty at rated power
Single pump covers all cases with PI control

Takeaways

The wind-turbine cooling model links analytical equations, MATLAB functions and Simscape components in one environment. It shows how cooler size, pump selection and valve strategy affect thermal margins and hydraulic performance before hardware is built, and the same framework can be reused for future turbine platforms.

Python Pressure-Drop Calculator

Interactive Jupyter dashboard for Darcy–Weisbach pressure calculations with editable roughness and K-factors.

Purpose and Scope

The notebook implements a quick design and comparison tool for single pipe runs. It calculates major, minor, and static pressure losses for one or more candidate pipe sizes, using the Darcy–Weisbach formulation.

Typical use cases:

• comparing diameter options for a new cooling or process line
• checking whether a pump has sufficient head for a given flow and elevation
• understanding the effect of fittings and valves via K-factors.

Governing Equations

For each selected pipe, the script applies the standard Darcy–Weisbach formulation. Volumetric flow Q in m³/h is converted to m³/s; the cross-section is A = π·D²/4, and the mean velocity is v = Q/A.

Reynolds number is Re = ρ·v·D / μ and the relative roughness is ε/D, where ε is entered in millimetres and converted to metres. The friction factor is:

• Laminar: f = 64 / Re
• Turbulent (Haaland):
f = [ −1.8·log10( (ε/D / 3.7)¹·¹¹ + 6.9/Re ) ]⁻²
• Auto: laminar below Re ≈ 2300, Haaland otherwise.

Major loss: ΔP_major = f·(L/D)·(ρ·v²/2)
Minor loss: ΔP_minor = K_total·(ρ·v²/2)
Static head: ΔP_static = ρ·g·Δz
Total: ΔP_total = ΔP_major + ΔP_minor + ΔP_static, reported as ΔP_bar = ΔP_total / 1e5 [bar].

Dashboard Layout and Workflow

The notebook uses ipywidgets to create a light dashboard-style UI:

• Documentation card summarising all steps and regimes
• Select pipes – up to three pipes with ID, material and roughness ε (mm)
• Constants – flow rate, pipe length, elevation, density, viscosity and friction model
• K-factors – element library (elbows, valves, tees, etc.) with quantity and running total K
• Run comparison – executes the calculation, prints a table and a bar chart, and exports a CSV file.

Materials, Roughness and K-Factors

A small library maps common pipe materials to typical absolute roughness values in millimetres (PVC/PE, stainless steel, commercial steel, cast iron, concrete, etc.). Selecting Custom (enter ε mm) unlocks the roughness field so datasheet values can be entered directly.

Minor losses are accumulated from a list of standard components. For each, the UI allows choosing the element type, editing its K-value if necessary, and defining the quantity in the line. The calculator maintains a table and chip showing the total K used in the Darcy–Weisbach minor-loss term.

Outputs

For each candidate pipe the tool reports:

• diameter, velocity and Reynolds number
• friction regime and Darcy f
• major, minor and static pressure losses in bar
• total pressure drop in bar.

A bar chart compares the total ΔP between selected pipes, and all numeric results are stored to pressure_drop_results.csv for documentation or further analysis.

End-Effector for High-Voltage Power Modules

Handheld / robotic contact tool for DC–DC converters and DC fast-charging power stages.

Project Context and Objectives

High-voltage power-electronic products, such as wall-mounted home-energy converters and DC fast-charging power stages, require a safe and repeatable way to connect test equipment to exposed copper busbars. Manual test leads are slow, operator-dependent and difficult to keep within creepage and clearance limits.

This project develops a dedicated end-effector that can be used either by an operator or by a robot. It must guide accurately onto the DUT’s copper terminals, make low-resistance electrical contact, respect all HV distances and survive thousands of docking cycles.

Reference Hardware

Design Requirements

Functional

• guide onto vertically oriented copper or plated terminals without side loading
• provide electrical continuity from DUT terminals to a rear connector
• tolerate ≈±0.5 mm lateral and ≈±1° angular misalignment
• allow one-hand operation or simple robotic motion.

Electrical / HV

• nominal current per pole up to 150 A DC (short periods), 100 A continuous
• DC link voltage up to 1000 V
• creepage / clearance between HV+ and HV− ≥ 22 mm in air
• target contact resistance < 100 µΩ per pole after endurance cycling.

Mechanical / Ergonomics

• total mass < 600 g
• no sharp metal edges near live parts or grip surfaces
• forks treated as sacrificial parts that can be replaced with standard screws.

Concept and Architecture

The end-effector is divided into front guiding forks, an insulating spacer block, a contact carrier, a rear electrical interface and a strain-relieved cable. The aluminium forks reference the DUT’s plastic guides but do not touch live copper directly. An FR-rated spacer block sets creepage distances and shields the user.

Behind the spacer, the contact carrier holds spring contacts and routes them to the rear connector. A cable gland and boot provide strain relief where the shielded harness exits the body.

Guiding Forks and Contacting Principle

Two U-shaped forks form the mechanical interface. Their tapered entries self-centre in the DUT’s plastic features; generous chamfers and rounded edges prevent damage to the guides.

Electrical contact is made by flat springs or leaf contacts inside the body. When the tool is fully seated, the springs land on the copper busbars with a defined normal force of about 15–20 N per pole, achieving low contact resistance while remaining compliant to small dimensional variations.

Electrical Interface

At the rear, a multipin connector groups power poles, protective earth / shield and low-level signals (voltage sense, temperature, interlock, communication). Multiple pins are paralleled for each power pole to keep current density within the connector rating and allow a compact package.

Applications and Validation

For the DC–DC converter the sequence is: dock, run continuity at low voltage, apply test power, execute functional tests, then undock. For the 1000 V fast-charging stage a safety interlock ensures that HV is only present when the tool is fully engaged and the cabinet is closed.

Validation includes dimensional checks of fork spacing, four-wire contact-resistance measurements, dielectric withstand tests and endurance cycling with inspection of both DUT and tool. The resulting design replaces a manual, operator-dependent HV connection with a controlled, automatable operation.

Design & Construction of PCBA Tester

Internship project at Tesla Giga Berlin · ECU ICT/FCT fixture with strain-safe touchdown

Project Context and Objectives

The assignment was to create a production-capable bed-of-nails tester for a new automotive ECU. The system combines In-Circuit and Functional tests with a dense field of spring-loaded probes while keeping board deformation at critical BGA corners within a defined principal-strain limit.

The fixture fits into an end-of-line station, complies with safety requirements, and provides highly repeatable touchdown without solder-joint damage. Strain gauges are used during validation; series production relies on the verified touchdown profile and documented process limits.

System Architecture

The design uses a clamshell layout. A moving top fixture carries the probe plate and travels vertically on four shafts with linear bearings, while a bottom fixture supports the ECU on a Delrin carrier. Gas springs generate the closing force and reopen the system when power is removed.

The probe plate is machined from G10/FR-4 for stiffness and electrical insulation. A primary locating pin defines the main datum; a slotted secondary pin removes rotation without over-constraining the plate, and spacer blocks set probe stand-off. The bottom fixture uses hardened bushings, guide pins, static pushers, and capacitive sensors to verify proper ECU seating before touchdown.

200+ spring probes for ICT and FCT
≤ 0.05 mm probe-plate deflection budget
≤ 400 µε principal-strain limit at BGA corner

Mechanical Design and Safety

The frame and base are aluminium for stiffness with acceptable mass. Linear guides constrain motion to the vertical axis so that probes experience primarily axial loading, and external dampers shape the final stroke to prevent touchdown bounce.

A risk analysis highlighted pinch points, stored energy in gas springs, and electrical hazards. The final system includes transparent pinch guards, interlocked access, an emergency-stop chain with safety relay, electromagnets that hold only at dwell, and a fail-safe return to the open position when power is lost.

FEM-Based Design Verification

Separate finite-element models were built for the probe plate and for the shaft/base structure. Only stiffness-relevant geometry was retained; material properties came from datasheets and mesh refinement kept changes in peak values below a few percent.

Under combined probe loads the G10 plate shows roughly 3.95 MPa von-Mises stress and about 0.026 mm maximum deflection. The shaft/base model predicts around 8.5 MPa peak stress and less than 0.006 mm displacement at the top-plate interface.

FoS ≈ 63 on probe-plate stress
FoS ≈ 1.9 on deflection limit
FoS > 11 on shaft stress screening limit

Strain-Gauge Strategy on the PCBA

To validate touchdown loads a 0°/45°/90° foil-gauge rosette is bonded at the corner of the main BGA, which is the dominant hot-spot for board bending and solder-joint failure. The rosette is centred on the corner land pad and solder removal is restricted to the minimum required footprint.

Gauge axes align with the board reference directions, and lead wires use strain-relief loops so that cable motion does not corrupt readings.

Data Acquisition and LabVIEW Application

Strain signals are acquired via an NI bridge-input module in quarter-bridge mode. LabVIEW converts mV/V outputs to strain using gauge factor and configuration, displaying live traces and principal-strain curves.

The software initialises and shunt-calibrates channels, zeros gauges with the fixture open, records data during approach and release, and logs peak values with full waveforms for offline analysis. Additional channels track actuator state so mechanical events can be aligned with strain peaks.

Results and Validation

Multiple ECUs were cycled through repeated touchdowns. The highest measured principal strain at the instrumented BGA corner is about 345 µε with a narrow spread between cycles, safely below the 400 µε limit.

FEM predicts roughly 360 µε at the same location; measurement and simulation agree within a few percent. No pad damage or electrical drift was observed, so the fixture is released for end-of-line use with documented gas-spring, damper, and travel settings.

Takeaways

The project combines fixture design, finite-element analysis, and direct strain measurement to tune a safe touchdown profile. The workflow—requirements capture, concept selection, simulation, rosette placement and LabVIEW-based validation—forms a reusable template for future ECU test systems.
