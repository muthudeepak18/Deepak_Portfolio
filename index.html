Design & Construction of PCBA Tester

Internship project at Tesla Giga Berlin · PCBA ICT/FCT test bench with strain-safe touchdown

1. Project Context and Objectives

The assignment was to develop a production-ready test bench for a new automotive ECU PCBA. The bench combines In-Circuit Test and Functional Test using about two hundred spring probes while keeping mechanical loading on the PCB within a 400 µε principal-strain limit defined for BGA packages.

The fixture had to fit into an end-of-line station, meet CE requirements, and deliver repeatable touchdown without inducing solder-joint damage. Strain measurement is used in the validation phase; series production uses the verified touchdown profile and process limits.

2. System Architecture

The system follows a clamshell concept. A moving top fixture carries the probe plate and slides vertically on linear bearings, while a bottom fixture supports and locates the ECU on a Delrin carrier. Gas springs provide closing force and automatically open the fixture when power is removed.

The probe plate is machined from G10/FR-4 for electrical insulation and stiffness. A primary locating pin defines the main datum; a slotted secondary pin removes rotation without over-constraining the plate. Spacer blocks set the probe-to-board stand-off. The bottom fixture uses hardened bushings, guide pins, static pushers, and capacitive sensors to guarantee repeatable seating of the ECU before touchdown.

3. Mechanical Design and Safety

The frame and base are aluminium for high stiffness and low mass. Linear guides constrain motion to the Z-axis so that probes see primarily axial load. External dampers shape the final part of the stroke and provide a slow, controlled touchdown without bounce.

A risk assessment identified pinch points, stored energy in gas springs, and electrical hazards. The final design includes transparent pinch protection, interlocked access, an emergency-stop chain with safety relay, electromagnets that hold only at dwell, and a fail-safe return to the open position when power is lost.

4. FEM-Based Design Verification

Finite Element models were created for the probe plate and for the shaft/base structure. Only geometry relevant for stiffness and stress was retained. Material properties came from datasheets, and mesh refinement studies kept changes in peak stress and deflection below five percent.

Under combined probe loads the G10 plate shows roughly 3.95 MPa von-Mises stress with a maximum deflection of about 0.026 mm. A separate model for the shafts and base predicts about 8.5 MPa maximum stress and less than 0.006 mm displacement under full load. These values fit well inside strength and alignment budgets.

FoS ≈ 63 on G10 plate stress
FoS ≈ 1.9 on plate deflection limit
FoS > 11 on shaft stress screening limit

5. Strain-Gauge Strategy on the PCBA

To validate touchdown loads, a 0°/45°/90° foil-gauge rosette is bonded near the corner of the main BGA. This position is the critical hot-spot for board bending and solder-joint damage, and matches layout guidance for board-level reliability.

The rosette is centred on the corner land pad; solder removal is restricted to the minimum area needed for bonding. Gauge axes follow the board reference directions, and the lead-out wires use strain-relief loops to avoid parasitic bending near the grid.

6. Data Acquisition and LabVIEW Application

Strain signals are captured by an NI 9237 bridge-input module in quarter-bridge mode. LabVIEW reads the channels, converts mV/V to strain using the gauge factor, and displays live traces for each rosette grid together with computed principal strains.

The application initialises and shunt-calibrates the channels, zeros the gauges with the fixture open, records data during approach and release, and logs peak values with full waveforms for later analysis. Additional channels track actuator state so that mechanical events line up with strain features.

7. Results and Validation

Ten ECUs were cycled five times each. The highest measured principal strain at the BGA corner is about 345 µε; the variation across all touchdowns stays within roughly ±10 µε. Even the largest transient remains below the 400 µε limit, leaving margin for component and process variation.

FEM predicts around 360 µε at the same location, which matches the measurements to within a few percent. No pad damage, cracking, or electrical drift was observed. The tester is released for end-of-line operation with specified gas-spring selection, damper settings, and touchdown travel.

8. Takeaways

The project combines clamshell-fixture design, FEM verification, and rosette-based strain measurement on the PCB. The resulting test bench delivers full ICT/FCT coverage while keeping engagement strain inside the specified limit, and the workflow is reusable for future ECU test systems.
